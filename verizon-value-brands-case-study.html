01 — Problem framing

The brief said "redesign the storefronts." That wasn't the real problem.

The brief I was handed and the brief I ended up solving were not the same document.

Verizon's prepaid portfolio — Total Wireless, Straight Talk, Simple Mobile, and four other value brands — had grown by acquisition. Each one had its own commerce stack, its own component library, its own conventions for something as basic as a plan card. The original ask from the account was scoped per-brand: improve Total Wireless's PDP, then look at Straight Talk's cart, and so on, as separate engagements.

I pushed back on that framing in the first week. Treating seven brands as seven unrelated redesigns meant seven times the design debt, seven different answers to the same UX question, and no way to ship a fix once and have it propagate. The actual problem wasn't "these storefronts feel dated." It was "this portfolio has no shared infrastructure for experience," which is a systems problem, not a page-by-page one.

"If I designed seven storefronts, I'd ship seven roadmaps. If I designed one system, I'd ship one decision — seven times, fast."
Brief as written

"Redesign the Total Wireless shop experience to improve conversion. Other brands may follow depending on results."

Brief as I reframed it to leadership

"Build one white-label experience system that ships brand-correct storefronts for all seven brands from shared components — so improving Total Wireless and improving Straight Talk become the same engineering and design effort."

The system, not the screen, was the deliverable

Constraints I had to design inside

  • Each brand carries distinct legal, pricing, and promo language that can't be templated away — regulatory copy differs by carrier sub-brand.
  • Engineering capacity was fixed; this had to ship faster than seven separate redesigns, not slower.
  • Brand teams each had their own stakeholders who needed to see their identity respected, not flattened into a "Verizon look."
  • No single source of truth existed yet for what "on-brand" meant per brand beyond a logo and a hex code.

Opportunities the reframe unlocked

  • A fix proven on one brand could de-risk the rollout for the other six — testing got cheaper, not more expensive, as we added brands.
  • Shared components meant UX research insight became portfolio-wide, not brand-locked.
  • A documented system gave Verizon a long-term asset, not just a one-time redesign — durable beyond our engagement.
02 — Process & iteration

The path bent twice. Both bends mattered more than the plan.

Insight-driven, not template-driven — each phase exists because the last one taught us something.
01Audit

Mapped every brand's PLP, PDP, plan grid, and checkout side by side

Not to find visual inconsistency — that was obvious — but to find which differences were intentional brand decisions versus accidental drift from no one owning the pattern. That distinction became the filter for everything after.

02First system pass

Designed a single "neutral" component set, themed only by color swap

The first version of the design system treated brand difference as skin-deep: same layout, same spacing, same interaction, different palette. It was the fastest path to a shippable system and engineering liked it for that reason.

What shifted it

Usability sessions on the Straight Talk plan grid showed customers anchoring on density and badge hierarchy differently than Total Wireless customers did — the "neutral" layout actually under-served both. Brand wasn't just color. It was information priority.

03Token-based rebuild

Rebuilt components so layout, spacing, and even badge logic could be governed by appearance tokens — not just color

This is the version that shipped: one component, any brand, where "brand expression" could touch more than a swatch — see the decision ledger below for how this got contested and resolved with engineering.

What shifted it

Once live, the gridwall engagement data made the cost of the rebuild legible — it wasn't an aesthetic preference, it was a measurable lift, which is what eventually got the heavier token system fully adopted, not just tolerated.

03 — Prototyping & communication

Prototypes were how we argued, not just how we presented.

Each fidelity level existed to answer a specific open question — not to look finished.
Low-fi — structure

Grayscale component maps

Used internally with engineering before any brand color touched the screen, specifically to separate "does this layout work" conversations from "does this look right" conversations, which kept critique sessions from collapsing into color debates.

Mid-fi — flow

Clickable PDP decision flow

Used in stakeholder reviews to simulate the device → plan → offer sequence end to end. This is what surfaced that customers were losing the pricing anchor once they reached storage/color options — directly shaped the "anchored pricing" pattern that shipped.

Hi-fi — brand proof

Token-swapped brand triplets

The same screen rendered three ways (Total, Straight Talk, Simple Mobile) side by side. This was the artifact that finally got engineering and brand stakeholders looking at the same evidence instead of arguing in the abstract.

"The hi-fi triplet wasn't a deliverable. It was the argument, made visible — three brands on one screen, so nobody had to imagine what flexible meant."
04 — Navigating complexity

The hardest problem wasn't a screen. It was where brand identity should live in the system.

Breaking one ambiguous, portfolio-wide question into decidable parts.

"Make it feel like each brand" is not a design spec — it's a direction. To make it decidable, I broke it into three separate questions that could each be answered with evidence rather than opinion, instead of treating "brand fidelity" as one big subjective judgment call.

Where does brand live: layout, color, or both?
Ruled out

Color-only theming — fastest to build, but usability testing showed it didn't carry real brand distinction for customers, only for stakeholders looking at swatches.

Chosen

Color + spacing + information density as brand-governed tokens, with interaction and behavior held constant across brands.

How much should brand teams customize vs. inherit?
Ruled out

Letting each brand team override components freely — this was the fastest path back to the original fragmentation, just with extra steps.

Chosen

Brand teams control tokens (appearance settings); core layout, behavior, and accessibility patterns stay locked at the system level.

What ships first to prove the model works?
Ruled out

Launching all seven brands simultaneously — too much risk surface to isolate what was working from what wasn't.

Chosen

Total Wireless first, instrumented closely, used as the evidence base to bring the remaining six brands along with less debate.

05 — Stakeholder navigation

Engineering wanted one component, period. I needed one component that could disagree with itself, by brand.

A real disagreement, resolved with evidence and shared cost — not authority.

Midway through the system build, engineering leadership proposed reusing components 1:1 across brands — same layout, same spacing, color swap only — to hit the speed-to-market target everyone had committed to. It was a reasonable position: less variable management, fewer QA paths, a simpler mental model for the whole team.

I disagreed, but I didn't have the authority to simply override it, and a flat "no" wasn't going to move a team that had its own delivery commitments. So I treated it as a question we could both get evidence on rather than a position to win.

Decision: how much should brand identity flex inside shared components? Resolved with eng + product + brand leads
Engineering's position — 1:1 reuse

One layout, one spacing system, color-only theming. Lower build cost, simpler QA matrix, faster to the speed-to-market number we'd all committed to.

Real risk: usability data I'd already gathered showed customers reading badge hierarchy and density differently by brand — this approach would ship a system that looked branded but didn't behave brand-correct.

What we aligned on — token-governed flexibility

I brought the usability findings, not an aesthetic argument — showed where the "neutral" layout was actually costing engagement on specific brands, using the same prototype triplets from the process above.

We agreed to scope flexibility narrowly: tokens could govern spacing, density, and badge logic, but interaction and accessibility patterns stayed fixed. That kept engineering's QA matrix close to their original estimate.

Trade-off owned

This cost more upfront — more variables to define, more documentation, a heavier component API than the 1:1 proposal. I owned that cost explicitly with engineering leadership rather than minimizing it, and we agreed to timebox the token-definition work so it had a hard ceiling instead of becoming open-ended scope.

What made this resolvable wasn't seniority — it was bringing engineering into the same evidence I was using, early enough that the conversation was "how do we scope this together" instead of "design wants more, engineering wants less." The gridwall engagement lift after launch became the retroactive proof that the heavier model was worth the build cost, but the alignment itself happened before we had that data, on the strength of the usability findings alone.

06 — Craft & design judgment

Quality, here, meant the system held up under pressure it didn't ask for.

What "craft" means on a multi-brand system — and where I chose speed over polish on purpose.
A

Craft is what survives the seventh brand, not how the first one looks

A beautiful Total Wireless screen that breaks when Simple Mobile's longer legal copy hits it isn't high craft — it's a one-brand demo. I treated every component as not-done until it had been stress-tested against the brand with the most extreme content: longest copy, most promo badges, densest plan grid.

B

The design system was a discipline, not a library

Components carried a strict rule: zero hardcoded values, everything bound to a token. That constraint was inconvenient in the moment — every new pattern had to earn its way into the token set rather than getting a one-off fix — but it's the only reason the system scaled to seven brands without seven divergent codebases.

C

Where I deliberately chose speed over polish

The CAI assistant panel shipped with a simpler visual treatment than the rest of the system — it was explicitly framed internally as an experimental surface, and polishing it further would have taken time away from hardening the checkout flow, where the conversion stakes were higher. Not every surface deserves equal craft investment; matching effort to stakes is itself a craft decision.

What I'd take into the next system

The biggest lesson wasn't about components — it was about when to introduce evidence into a disagreement. Bringing engineering the usability data before asking them to accept more scope changed the conversation from a negotiation into a shared problem. That's the move I'd repeat first on any multi-stakeholder system.
